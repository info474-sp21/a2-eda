Exploratory Data Analysis

The Dataset

The dataset I have selected is the Mental Health in Tech dataset from Kaggle. The dataset contains information about respondents, their employers, as well as their answers to a survey on mental health in the tech workplace. I hope to use this dataset to try to understand the attitudes towards mental health in the tech workplace.

Questions

  1. How does age play a role in mental health?
  2. How do attitudes towards mental health differ from physical health?
  3. Do employers provide employees with enough resources regarding mental health?
  4. Are people more comfortable with discussing a mental health issue with their coworkers or supervisors?

Phase 1: Understanding the Shape and Structure of the Dataset

The dataset contains 27 variables and 1,259 rows (records). Of the 27 variables, 22 of them are nominal/categorical. Because a majority of my variables are categorical/nominal, most of my visualizations will bar plots rather than scatter plots or line plots. One variable, Timestamp, is temporal. The Age and number of employees (no_employees) are the only two numerical variables. Two variables, Country and state, are geographical. The main variables I will be using to answer my questions are:

  • Age
  • treatment: Respondent sought treatment for mental health condition
  • work_interfere: If have a mental health condition, does it interfere with their work
  • mental_vs_physical: Does respondent feel that their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health
  • mental_health_interview: Would respondent bring up a mental health issue with a potential employer in an interview
  • phys_health_interview: Would respondent bring up a physical health issue with a potential employer in an interview
  • mental_health_consequence: Do you think that discussing a mental health issue with your employer would have negative consequences
  • phys_health_consequence: Do you think that discussing a physical health issue with your employer would have negative consequences?
  • benefits: employer provides mental health benefits
  • wellness_program: Has your employer ever discussed mental health as part of an employee wellness program
  • seek_help: employer provides resources to learn more about mental health issues and how to seek help
  • coworkers: Would respondent be willing to discuss a mental health issue with their coworkers
  • supervisor: Would you be willing to discuss a mental health issue with their supervisor

Phase 2: Investigating the Initial Questions

Visualizations 6 - 7: Q1 (How does age play a role in mental health?)

Visualization 6 - Work Interference of a Mental Health Condition by Age

To answer the first question, I wanted to see how age plays a role in work interference of a mental health condition. From the visualization, we clearly see that for a majority of people who are in the 20 to 40 binned age, a mental health condition most frequently interferes with their work sometimes, and there is a big gap between sometimes and the other categories (never, often , rarely). For people outside of the 20 to 40 binned age, the gap between sometimes and the other categories is very tiny, which indicates that there is a difference in work interference of a mental health condition by age.



Visualization 7 - Age Distribution of People who Did Seek Treatment and People who Did Not Seek Treatment for a Mental Health Issue

For this visualization, I wanted to see how age plays a role in whether people seek treatment for a mental health condition to help me answer the first question. Overall, the chart shows that more people sought treatment for their mental health issue than those who did not because the line for the 'Yes' category is higher than the 'No' category. The chart shows that far more people in the 20 and 40 age bins sought treatment than those who did not seek treatment. This is indicated by the large gap between the two lines at the 20 and 40 age bins. The frequency for those who did and did not seek treatment had smaller differences for people outside of the 20 and 40 age bins, due to the gap between the two lines getting smaller. This indicates that the rate for which people sought treatment decreased more dramatically as age increased.

Based on visualizations 7 and 8, we can see that age does play a role in mental health. To investigate the first question, I assessed mental health by using the work interference variable (If have a mental health condition, does it interfere with their work) and the treatment variable (whether respondents sought treatment for their mental health condition). I first compared the age variable by the work interference variable and then by the treatment variable. In both cases, we see that there are differences among the work interference variable and treatment variable by age, so we can conclude that age plays a role in mental health. Older people seek treatment for their mental health condition less frequently than younger people. Additionally, for people in their 20s to 40s, a mental health condition most frequently interferes with their work condition sometimes, while for other ages, a mental health condition sometimes and often interferes with their work condition.


Visualizations 8 - 9: Q2 (How do attitudes towards mental health differ from physical health?)

Visualization 8 - Distribution of whether respondents feel their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health

From the visualization, we can see that most respondents don't know whether their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health. Those who do believe and don't believe that their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health are pretty even.


Visualization 9 - First row of charts compare whether respondents would bring up a mental health issue compared to a physical health issue in an interview. Second row of charts compare whether respondents believe there would be negative consequences if discussing a mental health issue compared to a physical health issue with their employer.

From the first row of charts, we can see that the most frequent response to whether an employee would bring up a mental health issue in an interview is 'No'. However, when it comes to whether an employee would bring up a physical health issue in an interview, the most frequent response is 'Maybe'. This indicates that people are more likely to bring up a physical health issue in an interview than a mental health issue. Respondents are far less likely (more than twice less likely) to bring up a mental health issue in an interview compared to a physical health issue. From the second row of charts, we can see that far more people feel that there will not be any negative consequences of bringing up a physical health issue with their employer compared to a mental health issue.

Based on visualizations 8 and 9, we can see that there is a difference in how mental health and physical health are viewed. To investigate the second question and to assess the attitudes towards mental health and physical health, I first plotted a histogram of the mental vs physical variable, which measures whether respondents believe whether their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health. From the results, we see that most people don't know whether their employer takes mental health as seriously as physical health. Of those who do know, the number of people who do believe their employers take mental health as seriously as physical health are about the same number of people who do not believe their employers take mental health as seriously as physical health. To continue investigating the second question, I used the variables: mental_health_interview, phys_health_interview, mental_health_consequence, and phys_health_consequence. In visualization 9, I plotted the mental_health_interview and phys_health_interview variables in the first row and plotted the mental_health_consequence and phys_health_consequence in the second row. In doing so, I am able to see all four plots at once and be able to compare their frequencies. From the results, we can see that more people prefer not to bring up a mental health issue in an interview compared to a physical issue. Additionally, more people believe that there will be negative consequences if they bring up a mental health issue compared to a physical health issue. The attitude towards mental health issues is more negative and undisclosed than physical health issues in the workplace.


Visualization 10: Q3 (Do employers provide employees with enough resources regarding mental health?)

Visualization 10 - Three charts comparing whether employers provide mental health benefits, wellness programs, and resources to seek help for mental health issues

From the visualization, we can see that although some employers provide mental health benefits, many employers do not provide resources to learn more about mental health issues and how to seek help, and a majority of employers do not discuss mental health as part of an employee wellness program.

Based on visualizations 10, we can see that even though employers provide mental health benefits, employers do not provide employees with enough resources surrounding mental health. To investigate the third question and to assess the resources employers provide employees, I first plotted a histogram of the benefits to show how many employers provide mental health benefits to their employees. From the results, we see that more than a third of employers provide mental health benefits to their employees. To continue investigating the question, I plotted the seek_help variable and wellness_program variables next to the benefits plot all in one row so that I am able to see all three plots at once and be able to compare their frequencies and resources provided by the employers. From the results, we can see that almost half of employers do not provide wellness programs and resouces to learn more about mental health issues and how to seek help. Because of this, I believe that employers do not provide employees with enough resouces regarding mental health, in terms of wellness programs and resouces to learn more about mental health issues and how to seek help.


Visualization 11: Q4 (Are people more comfortable with discussing a mental health issue with their coworkers or supervisors?)

Visualization 11 - Comparing how comfortable people are with discussing a mental health issue with their coworkers or supervisor

From the visualization, we can see that most people people are willing to talk some of their coworkers, but this differs with supervisors. Surprisingly, more than double the number of people said yes to discussing a mental health issue with their supervisor than with their coworkers.

Analysis Process

Because my dataset has 22 categorical variables out of the 27 total variables, I knew that most of my plots would probably be histogram plots. For the first three visualizations, I wanted to explore how the age variable was distributed and whether the age variable contained any data issues since the age variable is my only numerical variable that I will be using to investigate one of my questions. I plotted my age variable as a histogram plot to view the age distribution and noticed there were impossibly large age values and negative values, so I had to transform my data by filtering out negative age values as well as very large age values so that I could create plots that use the age variable later. For my next two visualizations, I wanted to explore how one of the categorical variables were distributed, so I plotted a histogram of the work_interfere variable. I noticed that there was an 'NA' category, which indicates that the respondent didn't have a mental health condition, so I had to transform my data by filtering out 'NA' categories since I needed to investigate people who had a mental health condition.

For visualizations 6 and 7, I hoped to investigate my first question around how mental health is impacted by age. To assess mental health, I used the work interference of a mental health condition variable and the treatment variable and plotted each variable by the age. I wanted to see how work interference differed as age increased and how treatment differed as age increased. Because of this, I binned the age so that there weren't too many ages on the plot and plotted it on the x-axis so that I could see how treatment and work interference changed as age increased. For visualization 7, I made it a line chart so that I could see the distributions for the two categories for treatment (Yes, No) easily. I did not create a line chart for visualization 6 because there were 4 possible categories for the work interference variable, so creating a line chart would have cluttered up the chart, so I made it show points instead.

For visualizations 8 and 9, I wanted to investigate my second question around the how attitudes toward mental health and physical health differ. To assess mental health and physical health, I first used the mental vs physical variable, and plotted a histogram to see how people feel their employer view mental health compared to physical health. Then, I continued investigating the question by using the mental_health_interview, phys_health_interview, mental_health_consequence, and phys_health_consequence variables. For visualizstion 9, I plotted the mental_health_interview and phys_health_interview variables in the first row and plotted the mental_health_consequence and phys_health_consequence in the second row. In doing so, I was able to see all four plots at once and be able to compare their frequencies across mental health and physical health.

For visualization 10, I hoped to investigate my third question regarding mental health resources employers provide their employees. To assess the resources employers provide employees, I first plotted a histogram of the benefits to show how many employers provide mental health benefits to their employees. To continue investigating the question, I plotted the seek_help variable and wellness_program variables next to the benefits plot all in one row so that I was able to see all three plots at once and be able to compare their frequencies and resources provided by the employers. For my last visualization, I wanted to investigate my fourth question on whether people feel more comfortable discussing mental health issues with their coworkers or supervisors. To investigate this question, I used the coworkers and supervisor variables and created a histogram of each variable on plotted them side by side so that I could compare how frequently people would discuss their mental health issue with coworkers and supervisors.

Main Lessons Learned

The main lesson I learned when working with my dataset is that for numerical variables, it is important to make sure you know the minimum and maximum value of the variable. For instance, my age variable initially had a minimum of -1726 and a maximum value of 99,999,999,999. This would have really messed up my plots that used the age variable if I did not transform and filter my data. The most surprising insight I gained from my dataset is that more people are willing to definitely talk to their supervisor about a mental health issue than to their coworkers. Before I plotted the data to investigate this area, I thought that very few people would want to talk to their supervisors about a mental health issue, but that is not the case. For my other questions, I am not too surprised by my findings regarding mental health. For instance, my plots and findings confirmed what I had assumed about attitudes towards mental health and physical health; how mental health issues are brought up less frequently than physical health issues in the tech workplace. Fortunately though, we can see in visualization 10 that there are places where we can make improvements to help people with mental health issues in the workplace. More employers could try to provide resources to their employees to learn more about mental health issues and how to seek help as well as discussing mental health in employee wellness programs.
